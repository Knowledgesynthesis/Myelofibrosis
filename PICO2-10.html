Q3: Should JAK inhibitor-naïve, higher-risk (DIPSS intermediate-2 and high) overt Myelofibrosis (primary and post-ET/PV MF) patients without symptomatic splenomegaly and/or inflammatory symptoms be treated with JAK inhibitors vs not?

  1. Population: JAK inhibitor-naïve, higher-risk (DIPSS intermediate-2 and high risk) overt Myelofibrosis (primary and post-ET/PV MF) patients WITHOUT symptomatic splenomegaly and/or inflammatory symptoms
  2. Intervention: JAK inhibitors
    • Including: ruxolitinib, fedratinib, pacritinib, momelotinib
  3. Comparator: No JAK inhibitors
    • Comparators in studies will include placebo and best available therapy
    • (examples of BAT include hydroxyurea, prednisone, danazol, EPO stimulating agents, thalidomide, lenalidomide)
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • Leukemia (blast phase transformation) free survival
    • Molecular response
    • Hematological response
    • Adverse effects
    • QOL

Q4: Should JAK inhibitor-naïve, symptomatic lower-risk (DIPSS low, intermediate-1) overt Myelofibrosis (primary and post-ET/PV MF) patients be treated with JAK inhibitors vs not?

  1. Population: JAK inhibitor-naïve, symptomatic lower-risk (DIPSS low, intermediate-1) overt Myelofibrosis (overt primary and post-ET/PV MF) patients
    • Excluding intermediate-2 or high-risk patients
  2. Intervention: JAK inhibitors
    • Ruxolitinib, Fedratinib, Pacritinib, Momelotinib
  3. Comparator: No JAK inhibitors
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • Leukemia (blast phase transformation) free survival
    • Spleen response/spleen volume reduction
    • Symptom response: Quality of life/Symptom burden
    • Molecular response
    • Adverse events

Q5: Should JAK inhibitor-naïve, overt Myelofibrosis (primary, post-ET/PV) patients whose primary symptom burden is disease-related anemia be treated with anemia-directed therapy or JAK inhibitor therapy?

  1. Population: JAK inhibitor-naïve, overt Myelofibrosis (primary, post-ET/PV) patients with disease-related anemia as the primary symptom
    • We are excluding early/prefibrotic MF here
  2. Intervention: Anemia-directed therapy
    • Best available therapy will include erythropoietin, darbepoetin, prednisone, danazol, thalidomide, lenalidomide
  3. Comparator: JAK inhibitor therapy
    • Pacritinib and Momelotinib
    • (These JAK inhibitors have potential to improve hemoglobin.)
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • Leukemia (blast phase transformation) free survival
    • Anemia response
      1. Responses are defined in clinical trials, including rates of transfusion independence, clinical improvement in Hgb (>2g/dl in TI patients)
    • Symptom burden reduction/QOL
    • Adverse effects

Q6: Should patients with Myelofibrosis who experience JAK inhibitor-associated anemia be managed with second-line JAK inhibitors, compared to optimization strategies to maintain front-line therapy?

  1. Population: Patients with overt Myelofibrosis (overt primary, post-ET/PVMF) experiencing JAK inhibitor-associated anemia
    • Will not include early/prefibrotic MF
  2. Intervention: Second-line JAK inhibitors
  3. Comparator: Optimization strategies to maintain front-line therapy
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • Leukemia (blast phase transformation) free survival
    • Anemia response
    • Spleen response
    • Quality of life / Symptom burden
    • Adverse effects

Optimization strategies help manage anemia while maintaining the benefits of JAK inhibitor therapy, avoiding premature switching to second-line treatments. They can include dose modifications, addition of supportive medications, and transfusion support.

Q7: Should patients with higher-risk Myelofibrosis (int-2, high) on first-line JAK inhibitor therapy be referred for early transplantation, compared to delayed transplantation after failure of response to JAK inhibitor therapy?

  1. Population: Patients with higher-risk Myelofibrosis (DIPSS int-2, high) on first-line JAK inhibitor therapy
    • Focus on overt primary MF, also post-ET/post-PV MF
    • Not early/prefibrotic MF
  2. Intervention: Early referral for transplantation
  3. Comparator: Delayed referral for transplantation after loss of response or disease progression on JAK inhibitor therapy
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • Transplant-related mortality
    • Leukemia (blast phase transformation) free survival
    • Relapse

Q8: Among JAK inhibitor-naïve patients scheduled for transplant, should JAK inhibitor therapy be initiated prior to transplantation?

  1. Population: JAK inhibitor-naïve patients scheduled for transplantation
    • Overt primary and post-ET/PV patients with MF included here
  2. Intervention: Initiation of JAK inhibitor therapy prior to transplantation
  3. Comparator: No initiation of JAK inhibitor therapy prior to transplantation
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • Transplant-related mortality
    • Relapse
    • Acute GVHD
    • Chronic GVHD
    • Time to engraftment/cumulative incidence of graft failure
    • Infectious complications/viral reactivations

Engraftment and graft failure are opposite outcomes in stem cell transplantation. Engraftment is the successful establishment of transplanted stem cells, leading to blood cell production and immune system recovery, typically within 2-4 weeks. Graft failure occurs when transplanted cells fail to engraft or are lost after initial engraftment, leading to poor blood cell production and potential need for re-transplantation. The outcome measure “Time to engraftment/cumulative incidence of graft failure” assesses both the speed of engraftment and the proportion of patients experiencing failure.

Q9: Should aspirin therapy be used (or not) in all patients with Myelofibrosis?

  1. Population: All patients with Myelofibrosis, including subgroups:
    • 1) Prefibrotic 2) Overt (primary, post-ET/PV MF)
    • 2) JAK2 positive versus negative
  2. Intervention: Aspirin therapy
  3. Comparator: No aspirin therapy
  4. Outcomes:
    • Arterial thrombosis
      1. (CVA, TIA, ACS)
    • Venous thrombosis
      1. (DVT, PE, abdominal venous thrombosis, cerebral venous thrombosis)
    • Bleeding complications
      1. Clinically relevant non-major
      2. Major
    • Overall survival

Q10: Should overt Myelofibrosis patients with transfusional iron overload and elevated ferritin be treated with iron chelators or not?

  1. Population: Overt Myelofibrosis patients with transfusional iron overload and elevated ferritin that will NOT be transplanted
    • Will include overt primary and post-ET/PV MF patients
  2. Intervention: Treatment with iron chelators
  3. Comparator: No treatment with iron chelators
  4. Outcomes:
    • Overall survival
    • BP transformation/leukemia-free survival
    • Cardiac complications (e.g., CHF, arrhythmia)
    • Hepatic complications (e.g., cirrhosis, liver failure, hepatocellular carcinoma)
    • Anemia response
    • Adverse effects

The main iron chelators used for transfusional iron overload include:

  1. Deferasirox (Exjade, Jadenu)
  2. Deferoxamine (Desferal)
  3. Deferiprone (Ferriprox)
