Myelofibrosis

1. Overview of Myelofibrosis (MF)

  • Definition: A chronic myeloproliferative neoplasm characterized by bone marrow fibrosis, extramedullary hematopoiesis, and cytopenias.
  • Types:
    • Primary Myelofibrosis (PMF): Develops without a prior hematologic disorder.
    • Secondary Myelofibrosis: Evolves from Essential Thrombocythemia (ET) or Polycythemia Vera (PV).

2. Early (Prefibrotic) vs. Overt Myelofibrosis

  • Early/Prefibrotic Myelofibrosis:
    • Minimal bone marrow fibrosis (Grade 0-1)
    • Increased atypical megakaryocytes and granulocytic proliferation
    • Patients may be asymptomatic or present with mild anemia or thrombocytosis
  • Overt Myelofibrosis:
    • Advanced fibrosis (Grade 2-3) with significant collagen deposition
    • Cytopenias, extramedullary hematopoiesis, and constitutional symptoms
    • Includes Primary (Overt) MF and Secondary (Post-ET/Post-PV MF)

3. Blast Phase Transformation

  • Occurs when MF progresses to Acute Myeloid Leukemia (AML) (blast cells ≥20%)
  • Risk Factors:
    • Advanced age, high-risk mutations (e.g., ASXL1, SRSF2)
    • High blast count at diagnosis, unfavorable karyotype
    • Severe anemia, high-risk DIPSS score
  • Prognosis: Poor, with survival measured in months post-transformation

4. Mutational Landscape in Myelofibrosis

  • Standard Testing: Detects JAK2, CALR, MPL mutations
  • Extended Genetic Testing (NGS Panel): Identifies additional mutations affecting prognosis (e.g., ASXL1, EZH2, SRSF2, TP53)
  • Importance of Mutational Analysis:
    • Identifies high-risk mutations associated with aggressive disease
    • Helps predict disease progression and treatment response
    • Detects emerging subclones that could influence therapy

5. DIPSS (Dynamic International Prognostic Scoring System)

  • Risk Factors:
    1. Age > 65 years: 1 point
    2. Constitutional symptoms (fever, night sweats, weight loss): 1 point
    3. Hemoglobin < 10 g/dL: 2 points
    4. Leukocyte count > 25 x 10^9/L: 1 point
    5. Circulating blasts ≥1%: 1 point
  • Scoring System:
    • Low risk: 0 points
    • Intermediate-1: 1-2 points
    • Intermediate-2: 3-4 points
    • High risk: 5-6 points
  • Clinical Implications:
    • High-risk MF: Shorter survival (median 2-4 years), increased need for aggressive treatment (e.g., stem cell transplantation)
    • Low-risk MF: Longer survival (median 5-15 years), may require less aggressive management

6. Treatment Considerations

  • Early/Prefibrotic MF:
    • Observation or symptom-directed therapy.
    • Consideration for JAK inhibitors in symptomatic patients.
  • Overt MF:
    • JAK inhibitors (e.g., ruxolitinib, fedratinib) for symptom control.
    • Allogeneic stem cell transplantation for eligible high-risk patients.
    • Supportive care: Blood transfusions, erythropoiesis-stimulating agents (ESAs), thrombopoietin receptor agonists.
  • Blast Phase MF:
    • AML-directed therapy (e.g., hypomethylating agents, intensive chemotherapy, or targeted therapy).
    • Stem cell transplantation if feasible.
